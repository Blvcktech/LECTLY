Pharmacology — Drug Absorption

Medicine 45 min 6 sections
Edit Notes

Summary: This lecture covers the mechanisms of drug absorption in the GI tract, including passive diffusion, active transport, and factors affecting bioavailability.

1. Introduction to Drug Absorption Original

Drug absorption refers to the process by which a drug moves from its site of administration to the bloodstream. The rate and extent of absorption depend on the drug's physicochemical properties, the dosage form, and the physiology of the absorption site.

Key Points:

Oral administration is the most common route for drug delivery
Absorption primarily occurs in the small intestine due to large surface area
Drug must cross at least one biological membrane to reach systemic circulation
Bioavailability: The fraction of an administered drug that reaches systemic circulation in unchanged form.

Learn: Drug Absorption

Beginner Intermediate Advanced
Step-by-Step Explanation

When you swallow a pill, it travels to your stomach and then your small intestine. The drug molecules need to pass through the intestinal wall and into your blood vessels. This is called absorption. The small intestine is the main site because it has a huge surface area — about the size of a tennis court when unfolded.

Real-World Analogy

Think of your intestine as a security checkpoint at an airport. The drug molecules are passengers trying to get through. Some walk right through (passive diffusion), while others need a special escort (active transport). The more gates open, the faster everyone gets through — that's why the small intestine, with millions of "gates" (villi), is so efficient.

Examples
Aspirin is absorbed mainly by passive diffusion in the stomach (acidic environment)
Iron supplements use active transport (carrier-mediated)
